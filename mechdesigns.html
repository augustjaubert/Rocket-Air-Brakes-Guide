Slow & Easy Actuation
Flaps deployed against the airflow will experience the highest force on actuation from high velocities. This means they will require large actuators or gear arrangements such as worm gears or lead screws. The use of such arrangements however means that the activation will be much slower.
Highest Drag Potential
As surface flaps have the largest surface area capability, they allow for the design to have a large drag potential.
Heavy
While allowing for a higher drag potential, this means the system’s structure will have to support a larger force. Material considerations may come from the fact that it will need heavier structural components such as metals.
Large Volume
Additionally, as the surface area of the flaps increases, so does the volume of the system. While it would be possible in theory to place other components in between the mechanism, that is not recommended.
Slow & Easy Actuation
Brakes extending normal to the airflow don’t overstrain the motor by forcing them to counteract the resisting force directly and typically allow them to fully deploy with a smaller motion. While it is dependent on your design, they typically require simple mechanisms.
Low Drag Potential
The drag potential of these air brakes relies entirely on the cross-sectional area of the rocket. They can’t exceed the maximum area offered, and therefore their drag potential is usually lower (if not substantially lower) than others.
Lighter
Extension brakes are placed like a plate inside the rocket, just like you would do to separate the sections. This means they are generally quite compact as they don’t take up much space (the electronics bay will be the one taking up the most space).
