Air brakes are a type of flight control surface mounted on a vehicle to increase its drag without significantly changing its lift, allowing for the vehicle to decelerate.

In many high power rocketry competitions, one of the grading criteria is based on achieving a predefined target altitude at apogee. The rocket flight can be separated into four stages: Launch and powered ascent, coasting period up to apogee, descent and landing. In the case of a model rocket braking system, only the launch and coasting stages are of interest.

After reaching motor burnout, a rocket mostly follows a ballistic trajectory. While methods to control the apogee can be achieved through very precise flight simulations and adapting the rocket accordingly (changing the mass of the rocket with a dummy mass), they donâ€™t implement a control system and cannot adjust depending on its environment.

If you plan on launching a rocket at higher altitudes (>1km), it gets trickier to make the right assumptions in order predict the flight trajectory. Instead, you could rely on the telemetry measurements of your rocket so that it can know in real time where it is in space, how it is orientated, and hopefully be smart enough to figure out where it is headed.

The two main type of air brakes used in high-power rocketry are:

1. Surface Flap Brakes

They physically move the surface of the body tube, turned into concentric flaps, in order to deviate the airflow.

Note

Depending on the design it can move the airflow towards the inside of the rocket (if open to the flow) and create some extra drag that is unaccounted for.

Additionally, if they are made from the body tube (a modular design could have whole body section as flaps), the amount of body surface circumference used for the flaps can cause concern for the structural integrity.

2. Extension Brakes

The more simplistic approach. A platform extends normal to the airflow through openings in the body tube.

Note

Depending on their size (in terms opening sizes required to allow them to pass through the body tube), the openings could act as stress concentrators and compromise the structural integrity of the rocket.

Air Braking Approaches
Actively Modulating System
A modulating system allows you to control the amount of drag you want to add to the rocket by varying the degrees of deployment (or %) to choose from. Additionally, a closed loop control can be implemented.
Single-use System
A single-use brake system has two states (un-deployed and fully deployed) and fully deploys the air brakes once at a specific timing decided by the program. This type of control would require a much more reliable and precise prediction model, but can be chosen for its simplicity and small size in non-competitive flights.

The main criteria you would want your air brakes to fulfill are,

Lightweight
As mass is a central issue in high-power rocket, the lighter a system can be, the more beneficial it is to its performance (within the limits of its structural integrity).
Compact
A compact design leaves more room for other components, and also allows a rocket to be designed closer to the desired dimensions.
Simple and Fast Actuation & Modulation
Faster actuation and modulation speeds allow for better control of vehicle drag. As a general rule, simpler mechanisms are obviously easier to operate and leave less room for error, which is one the main goals to strive for.
